Presentation

Carthage was a powerful and influential Phoenician city-state founded in the 9th century BC on the coast of present-day Tunisia. Renowned for its strategic location and exceptional maritime capabilities, Carthage became a dominant force in the Mediterranean region. Its advanced trade networks, innovative shipbuilding, and thriving economy made it a key player in ancient history. However, Carthage is perhaps most famous for its series of conflicts with Rome, known as the Punic Wars, which ultimately led to its destruction in 146 BC. Despite its fall, Carthage’s legacy as a center of culture, commerce, and military power remains significant in the annals of history.

Principal Vestiges

Byrsa Hill

The heart of Carthage, this area served as the city’s citadel. Today, it houses remnants of Punic buildings and the Cathedral of Saint Louis, as well as the Carthage National Museum, which showcases artifacts from the Punic and Roman eras.

Tophet of Carthage

A sacred site thought to have been used for ritual sacrifices and burials during the Punic period. It contains numerous urns and stelae, offering insights into Carthaginian religious practices.

Punic Ports

The remains of the ancient naval and commercial harbors, which were vital to Carthage’s maritime power. These ports demonstrate the city’s strategic and economic prowess.

Antonine Baths

One of the largest Roman bath complexes in Africa, built during the Roman era. This grand structure reflects the Roman transformation of Carthage into a provincial capital.

Roman Amphitheater

A well-preserved amphitheater that hosted gladiatorial games and other public events. It exemplifies Roman influence on the city post-conquest.

Magma Reservoirs

Ancient cisterns that were part of a sophisticated water supply system, ensuring the city's survival during sieges.

Sanctuary of Baal

An ancient religious site dedicated to the deity Baal, reflecting the spiritual life of the Punic people. The site includes altars and inscriptions offering rich historical insights.

Roman Aqueducts

A marvel of ancient engineering, these aqueducts brought water from distant springs to the city of Carthage, showcasing Roman expertise in infrastructure.

Major Historical Events

Founding of Carthage (9th Century BC)

Carthage was founded by Phoenician settlers from Tyre, led by Queen Dido. Its strategic location along the Mediterranean made it a hub of trade and commerce, laying the foundation for its future as a powerful city-state.

The Rise of a Maritime Empire (7th–5th Century BC)

Carthage expanded its influence across the Mediterranean, establishing colonies in North Africa, Sicily, Sardinia, and Spain. Its advanced naval fleet secured dominance in trade and maritime warfare.

The Punic Wars with Rome (264–146 BC)

Carthage engaged in three major conflicts with Rome, known as the Punic Wars. The most notable of these was the Second Punic War, during which the Carthaginian general Hannibal led a daring campaign through the Alps. Ultimately, the Third Punic War ended with the complete destruction of Carthage in 146 BC.

Roman Conquest and Reconstruction (146 BC–44 BC)

Following its destruction, Carthage was rebuilt as a Roman colony by Julius Caesar. The city regained prominence as a center of agriculture, trade, and culture under Roman rule.

Vandal Invasion (5th Century AD)

During the decline of the Roman Empire, Carthage was captured by the Vandals in 439 AD. It became the capital of the Vandal Kingdom, marking a period of instability.

Byzantine Reconquest (6th Century AD)

In 533 AD, the Byzantine general Belisarius recaptured Carthage during the reign of Emperor Justinian I, restoring it as a provincial capital of the Byzantine Empire.

Islamic Conquest (7th Century AD)

Carthage fell to the Arab forces in 698 AD, marking the end of its significance as a major urban center. Tunis eventually rose to prominence in the region, overshadowing ancient Carthage.
